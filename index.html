Build & ship a real app — with AI as your co-builder

Your final project is to take everything we practiced this semester and use it to design, build, and publish a small working web app that solves a real problem. By the end you'll have a live link, on the internet, that anyone can open.

No coding background needed Built by vibe-coding Published on GitHub Pages

01What you're building

A small web app — something a person can open in a browser and actually use.

This is not an essay about AI. It's a thing that works. A calculator, a planner, a converter, a quiz, a checklist tool, a tracker, a generator — whatever solves a problem you (or someone you know) genuinely has. It can be about anything; it does not have to be "serious." It just has to do its one job well and be live on the internet when you submit it.

You will build it the way we built our in-class apps: by describing what you want to an AI in plain language, looking at what it makes, telling it what's wrong, and repeating until it's good. This is what people call vibe-coding — you bring the judgment and the taste, the AI writes the code.

The one rule that matters

Two things decide whether this project succeeds: (1) the app actually works, and (2) it is live at a public link. Everything else in this guide exists to get you to those two things. A simple app that works and is online beats an ambitious app that's broken or stuck on your laptop — every time.

02The workflow, start to finish

Seven steps. The first six are the skills you already practiced this semester; the seventh — publishing to GitHub — is the new piece, and it's spelled out in full in Section 03.

1

Think

Find a problem worth solving

Start with a real itch, not a cool idea. Ask yourself: what small task is annoying, repetitive, or confusing — for me, my family, my studies, or my work? Then narrow it to one user and one job.

  • Who is it for? "Someone studying for a driving test." "A parent splitting a restaurant bill." "Me, tracking my water intake."
  • What is the one job? Say it in a single sentence: "It lets you ____ so that you can ____."
Tip

Smaller is better. A tool that does one thing cleanly is a great project. A tool that tries to do five things usually does none of them.

2

Shape

Shape the idea in conversation with the AI

Before asking for any code, use the AI as a thinking partner — exactly the context-building and step-by-step prompting we practiced. Describe your user and the one job, then ask it to help you sharpen the plan:

  • "Here's my idea: [describe it]. Who would use this and what would they expect it to do?"
  • "What is the simplest possible version that's still useful? What should I leave out for now?"
  • "Poke holes in this. What will confuse a first-time user?"

Come out of this conversation with a short, clear description of the app and a list of its core features. That description becomes your build instructions and the seed of your README.

3

Build

Vibe-code the first version

Now hand the AI the plan and ask it to build it. Be explicit that you want something you can run in a browser:

# A prompt that works well:
Build me a simple web app that does the following: [paste your
description and feature list]. It should run in a web browser with
no installation. Keep everything in as few files as possible.
Explain how to open and run it.

The AI will produce the files (often a single index.html, sometimes with separate JavaScript and CSS). Open it and see it run. It will not be perfect. That's expected — that's what Step 4 is for.

Tip

If your tool gives you a "preview" or "open in browser" button, use it. If it just gives you files, save them in one folder and double-click index.html to open it in your browser.

4

Iterate

Iterate until it doesn't break

This is the loop you know well: look → describe what's wrong → let the AI fix → look again. Talk to it like a person, not in code:

  • "When I leave the field empty and click the button, nothing happens. It should show a friendly message."
  • "The result is correct but it's hard to read. Make it bigger and put it in a box."
  • "Try breaking it: what happens if someone types text where a number should go?"

Deliberately test the edge cases — empty inputs, silly inputs, very large numbers. An app that doesn't crash when used "wrong" is the mark of a finished project.

5

Polish

Make it look intentional

You don't need to be a designer — you need to ask for good design. A few prompts go a long way:

  • "Make this look clean and modern. Use clear headings, comfortable spacing, and a simple color scheme."
  • "Make sure it looks good on a phone screen, not just a laptop."
  • "Add a short title and one line explaining what the app does at the top."

Aim for looks pleasant, reads clearly, nothing cluttered. That's the bar.

6

Document

Write the README

A README (pronounced "read me") is the front page of your project — a short text file named README.md that tells a stranger what your app is and how to use it. You don't have to write it alone: hand your app and your Step 2 description to the AI and ask it to draft one. A good README has just five things:

# App Name One sentence: what it does and who it's for. ## What it does A short list of the main features. ## Live link https://your-username.github.io/your-app/ ## How to use it Open the link and… (1–3 simple steps). ## Known limitations Be honest: "Only works for X", "doesn't yet handle Y".
Tip

Listing your app's limitations isn't a weakness — it shows you understand your own project. Always include this section.

7

Ship

Publish it to GitHub Pages

This is the step that turns "files on my laptop" into "a link anyone can open." It's the one genuinely new skill in this project — so it gets its own section, with every click spelled out. Go to Section 03 below.

03Publishing to GitHub Pages — the full walkthrough

GitHub is a free home for projects. GitHub Pages is a free feature that turns your project files into a live website. Two parts: first get your project onto GitHub (Part 1), then switch Pages on (Part 2). None of it requires writing code — a desktop app or your browser does the work.

What you need first

A folder on your computer containing your app's files — at minimum a file named exactly index.html, plus any JavaScript, CSS, or image files it uses, and your README.md. If your app has all its code inside a single index.html, that one file is enough.

Part 1 — Get your project onto GitHub

Two ways to do this. Pushing your project with the free GitHub Desktop app is the recommended route — a few minutes to set up once, and after that every update is a single click. Drag-and-drop upload (Route B) is a no-install fallback if you get stuck.

Route A — Push with GitHub Desktop recommended

1

Install GitHub Desktop and sign in

Download it free from desktop.github.com, install it, and sign in. If you don't have a GitHub account yet, create one at github.com first — choose a username you're happy to have in your public link, since it becomes part of your web address (a user named danacohen gets links like danacohen.github.io/...).

2

Add your app folder

In GitHub Desktop choose File → Add Local Repository and select the folder with your app in it. If it warns that the folder isn't a repository yet, click the "create a repository" link it offers, keep the default settings, and click Create Repository. This turns your folder into a project GitHub can track.

3

Commit your first version

Your files appear in the left panel. In the Summary box at the bottom-left, type a short note like "First version", then click Commit to main. A "commit" is simply a saved snapshot of your project.

4

Publish (push) to GitHub

Click Publish repository at the top. Make sure "Keep this code private" is unticked so it stays public, then click Publish repository. Your project is now on GitHub. (Pushing just means sending your saved snapshot up to GitHub.)

5

Push your updates as you go

Every time you change your app afterwards, GitHub Desktop shows exactly what changed. Type a short summary, click Commit to main, then click Push origin at the top. That's the whole loop for the rest of the project — commit, push, done.

Already working in a terminal?

If you're comfortable with the command line — or your AI is already running commands for you — you can skip the app entirely. Ask your co-builder: "Walk me through pushing this folder to a new public GitHub repository with git." It will give you the exact git init → git commit → git push commands and explain each one.

Route B — Drag-and-drop upload fallback

No installs — everything happens in your browser. Use this only if GitHub Desktop won't cooperate.

1

Create a repository on the website

At github.com, click the + in the top-right corner → New repository. Give it a short, lowercase name with no spaces (for example bill-splitter), set it to Public, tick "Add a README file", and click Create repository.

2

Upload your app files

On the repository page, click Add file → Upload files. Drag your files from your computer into the browser window — your index.html and everything next to it — then scroll down and click Commit changes. Your index.html must sit at the top level of the repository, not inside a sub-folder. To update later, repeat this with the new version (a file with the same name replaces the old one).

Part 2 — Switch on GitHub Pages

This part is identical no matter which route you used to get your files up.

1

Turn on Pages

On your repository, click Settings (top menu) → Pages (left sidebar). Under "Build and deployment" → "Source", choose Deploy from a branch. Under Branch, select main and the folder / (root), then click Save.

2

Get your live link

Wait about 1–2 minutes, then refresh the Pages screen. A green box appears: "Your site is live at …" with a link like https://your-username.github.io/bill-splitter/. Click it to check it works. That public link is your submission.

3

Put the link in your README

Add your live link to README.md under the Live link heading and save. Now anyone who finds your project can reach the working app. GitHub Pages refreshes your live site automatically within a minute or two whenever you push or upload a change.

If your app has a "build step"

Some apps (like our in-class Marginless tool) use extra tooling and must be "built" first — a command like npm run build turns your project into a finished folder, usually named dist or build. If that's you: run the build (ask your AI to walk you through it), then push or upload the contents of that folder — not the whole project — so index.html ends up at the top level. Pages settings and the live link work exactly the same.

When in doubt, ask your co-builder

If anything here gets stuck, paste the exact screen or error into your AI and say: "I'm trying to publish my app to GitHub Pages and I'm stuck here: [describe what you see]. Walk me through the next click." Deployment trouble is one of the easiest things for the AI to talk you through.

Before you hand it in, walk this checklist. If every box is true, you're done.

A public GitHub repositoryYour project lives in a public repo, with your index.html and any supporting files.
A live GitHub Pages link that opens and worksOpen it in a fresh browser tab (or on your phone). The app loads and does its job. This is the single most important item.
A README in the repoIncludes the five parts: what it is, what it does, the live link, how to use it, and known limitations.

05Common pitfalls & FAQ

The small things that trip people up — and how to get past them.

The AI wrote the code. Is that allowed?

Yes — that's the entire point. This course is about directing AI well. Your work is the thinking: choosing a real problem, scoping it, judging the result, fixing what's wrong, and shipping it. That's the skill being assessed.

My app works on my laptop but the live link shows a blank page.

Almost always one of three things: (1) your file isn't named exactly index.html; (2) index.html is inside a sub-folder instead of at the top level of the repo; or (3) the app has a build step and you uploaded the source instead of the built dist folder (see Route B). Paste the problem into your AI and it'll pinpoint which one.

How "good" does the app have to be?

It has to work, be live, and do one real job cleanly. A modest tool that nails that is a strong project. Ambition is welcome but never at the cost of "it's broken" or "it's not online."

My idea feels too big and I'm getting lost.

Cut it down. Ask the AI: "What's the smallest useful version of this?" and build only that. You can always add more later — but a finished small app beats an unfinished big one.

Does my app need to connect to the internet or use an outside service?

No. Most great projects run entirely in the browser with no outside data. If you do use a free public service, make sure the app still behaves gracefully when it's unavailable — and never paste a private password or secret key into your code, because your repository is public.

What language should the app be in?

English. Your app's on-screen text, your README, and your repository should all be in English — not Hebrew. Make sure the wording reads cleanly and the layout looks right.

Do I need a PRD or any formal planning document?

No. Your README is the only document required. The planning happens in your conversation with the AI (Step 2) — keep it light and focused on building.
